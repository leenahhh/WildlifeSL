Leopard of Sri Lanka


National Parks in Sri Lanka to See Leopards



Kumana National Park


Situated in the southeast region of Sri Lanka, the 18,149-hectare Kumana National Park is a renowned destination for eco-tourists and a sanctuary for birds. Yala National Park's eastern region is known as Kumana National Park.

The "Kumana Villu," a 200 hectare natural swamp lake fed by the "Kumbukkan Oya" via a half-mile-long, narrow channel, is one of the most notable features of the Kumana National Park.

In addition to the abundant avian population, Kumana National Park harbors several mammal species that may be found in the broader Yala National Park, including leopards and elephants.



Wilpattu National Park


Stretching across more than 131 thousand hectares, this protected area is the largest and oldest in Sri Lanka.

The Department of Wildlife Conservation oversees the five blocks that make up the park, which was established between 1938 and 1973 in accordance with the Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance. WNP borders the districts of Mannar and Vavuniya and extends over Puttalam and Anuradhapura.

In addition to being one of the most significant habitats for elephants in the nation, the location is excellent for seeing sloth bears and Sri Lankan leopards. Compared to many other Sri Lankan national parks, it has a denser forest cover.

The scenery of the WNPs is made up of sand dunes, vast open plains, distinctive Villu wetlands, and dry zone woods and prickly scrub. The bare reddish cliffs that rise sharply from the slender beaches of the Dutch Bay and Portugal Bay are another remarkable aspect of the area.



Yala National Park


Shaggy undergrowth and monsoon woods predominate in Yala, which is situated in the island's arid zone.

The reserve is primarily level, but it is broken up by large granite outcrops, meadows, and brackish lakes. Sand dunes define the reserve's coastline edge.

The park receives rainfall from the northeast monsoon and is located in the island's semi-arid zone.


Threats to Leopards in Sri Lanka




Excessive Huntings



Poachers occasionally target leopards for the illegal wildlife market trading of their skin, bones, and other body parts. This illicit activity is a serious danger to Sri Lankan leopard populations.

The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) has designated the Sri Lankan leopard, a native subspecies of leopards, as endangered. There are only about 1000 leopards in the world, and poaching is one of the many factors contributing to their diminishing population. These days, the population is restricted to those parts of the nation that respect the preservation of these animals.

Since 1964, the Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance has provided protection for Sri Lankan leopards. Leopards are now classified as "strictly protected species" under the 2009 modification to the Fauna and Flora Protection Ordinance.

This means that it is illegal to kill, maim, or hurt a leopard, or to keep a live animal, a dead body, or any part of a body. Selling or exposing a dead body or any part of one, as well as dealing in live animals, are prohibited when it comes to leopards.

The use of any tool, material, or device to carry out any of these offenses is included in the offenses. Additionally, each of these offenses is considered a cognisable offense, meaning that an arrest warrant is not necessary to apprehend the perpetrator. Regular research is also conducted to improve Sri Lankan leopard conservation efforts.

Loss of Habitat



The loss of habitat brought on by human activities like deforestation, urbanization, and agricultural development is one of the main dangers facing leopards in Sri Lanka. There is less room for leopards to wander and find food as human populations increase and encroach upon natural areas. This causes conflicts with humans and a fall in leopard population.

Wild animals are forced into increasingly constrained areas as a result of ad hoc initiatives that are started, frequently at the request of local politicians, and the destruction of forests and natural habitat.

They invade cultivations that were formerly their home areas since they are unable to thrive on the food that is there, and their demise is certain. The prey species of leopards migrate towards humans first, attracted by the nutrient-rich cultivations and with little remaining in their current confinement regions. The local population hunts them down, making them the main targets of snares and traps.

Following them is inevitable, and once they do, the leopards find domestic animals simpler to kill than their more cunning wild cousins due to their diminished survival instincts.

It is a blessing that so many non-governmental organizations, academic institutions, and even concerned individuals put up so much effort to preserve what is left. The first Multi-Regional Monitoring System for the Conservation of the Sri Lanka Leopard was introduced in the nation in 2021 by the WNPS and LOLC. Six research stations were first established in Kotagala, Sinharaja, Panama, Kilinochchi, Belihul Oya, and Sigiriya. The research will monitor the presence of leopards using video traps and surveys.

Along with acting as information hubs for the local community, the research centers will communicate with the DWC and Forest Officers and develop research-based strategies for mitigating human-leopard conflicts. In order to combat the rising number of leopard deaths in Sri Lanka, this is essential.

Another such endeavor is the launch of much-needed awareness campaigns by Unilevers and the Rainforest Alliance in the upcountry plantation areas where the number of human-leopard conflicts has rapidly increased.
